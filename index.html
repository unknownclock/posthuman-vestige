Post-Human Vestige

Is...?

Is this it?






Is this really how it ends?




Snowflakes glitch in and out of my vision like stuck pixels on a screen.
My heart sputters on like a decrepit engine.

My limbs keep locking up, freezing like a system crash.

Heat escapes from every pore on my body, like a failing cooling system, each vent pushing out more and more warmth.

Wait, no. Not a cooling system.

I’m not a machine.

No matter how much easier things would be if I were.

Could I have avoided this if I were?

I can feel myself atrophying, blood seeping through intricately woven cloth—responsive materials and smart technology failing to mend the weeping wound at my side.

Every cool breath burns my already overworked throat.

It’s not enough.

I fight the drag in my limbs.

The trees stretch towards the sky.

I don’t need to look at my wound to know—I’m dying.

Despite the hundreds of different deaths I spent minutes, hours, days imagining, I hadn’t imagined this one.

I’d imagined plane crashes, terrible freak accidents like falling down the stairs or getting an infection when my foot had caught onto a rusty nail.

I’d spent days after that one in particular, hazy images of my body locking up and seizing as disease spreading through my body like a computer virus printed on the backs of my eyelids.

I’m leaving sinkholes of warm blood behind.

My brain begins to whir with all the possibilities of what happens now.

Are there wolves in this forest?
I don’t know.
How many wolves are even still on the planet? They were added to the Endangered Species list in 2045.
I wouldn’t have to worry about this if I’d stayed inside, why why didn’t I stay inside?
But wait—what does that matter?
The Synths or Androids will probably find me first.

Each new thought pops up like a new computer window that consumes more and more memory, more space in my overloaded head.

And now a new death is conjured, the Synths catching me and playing with me, injuring me like a brown bear playing with its food, only playing dead wouldn’t work on their artificial eyes.

Everything, from my temperature, and the rapid pump of blood through my stiffening body, even the amount of heat loss being lost from me would be recorded and transmitted as high-frequency radio signals to a microchip implanted in the retina of their bionic vision systems.

Each new step becomes more sluggish than the last, and each of my joints creak just a bit louder.

I didn’t expect to die this way.

I mean, I had imagined it a hundred, a thousand different times.

This gaudy, oppressive techno-jungle—where humans had long since dropped to just 24% of the population—expected a level of perfection only machines could provide.

Despite that, we still tried to hold onto our limited job prospects.

We were mostly kept around as fleeting bits of entertainment.

There for nostalgia, prosperity, and for pity.

After all, with Synths, death had been delayed.

With androids, death had been eradicated.

Life was long for Synths and constant for Androids.

They would outlive us.

Their memories are recorded and stored as high-resolution audio and visual information stored in high-quality processors. Neural networks had been merged with advanced computational systems. Bio-Synthetic Composite Skin made to look however the Synths wanted.

Compared to the fleeting specks of life we clung to, we were nothing.

We were outdated. We were the Macintosh to their Macs and laptops.

Perhaps that’s why they kept us around.

To confirm their own superiority. To pretend they had somehow maintained their humanity in their new, unblemished forms.

When Synths first came on the rise, they were promoted as a way of escaping imperfect bodies.

Diseases, sickness, mental illnesses—gone.

Cancer? Nonexistent.

They were humans just repackaged, they said.

And then death, too, became a concept rather than a reality.

Synths were made more and more out of synthetic material and less and less of the original meaty, ephemeral flesh. At least for the Synths.

For the stragglers of humans who were left, it remained the great equalizer.

The AI revolution, against all assumptions, was quiet.

The march to our end is a distant hum, drowned out in a world already deafened by noise.

There was no dystopian takeover. No guns, no nuclear bombs, nothing flashy or dramatic.We made it easy.

We fed them our books, our movies, our thoughts, our work, our money, our minds.

After so many wars, and so much death and so much human error, we traded freedom and conscious thought for convenience.

We were flawed, but AI learned and grew, fattening up on the milk of human knowledge and transcending it.

It was perfect, and we hung on their every word like the gospel.

We revered them, praising their knowledge, going to it when we were stuck or lost.

AI was perfect, and then we made them in our image.

And then they made us into theirs.

AI programs snowballed into robots, which snowballed into androids, which snowballed into Synths.

Memories, appearance, and biological data encoded and transmitted into fusions of carbon fiber composites, steel, and artificial organs.

In droves, people abandoned their flawed bodies for the clean, perfect forms of Synths.

After worshipping AI and tech like gods for so long, is it any surprise that we clawed at the opportunity to become more like them?

Humans created their replacements.

Some even became their replacements.

But—are you still human if every original part of you has been replaced?

August 28th, 2045.

The day Synth testing had been completed.

The day humanity, in the sense of being human, had signed its own death warrant.

They outnumbered us so quickly.

The androids.

The Synths—especially the Synths.

Each new Synth meant one less human body in the world.

Viscera replaced by nanotech and steel.

The day the President of _______ died, the drums of death had gotten louder.

It wasn’t the death itself that mattered.

It was what happened after.

He was the last human president.

And the first Synth one.

I was 10 years old when it happened.

I remember sitting in my grandmother’s living room, an old-style flat-screen TV blaring one of his live speeches.

By then, the human population had dropped to 37%.

And it was still plummeting.

Humans were dying faster and faster, finding new ways to disappear.

The collapse of human society had long begun and was reaching its fever pitch.

That’s why I stopped going outside.

I spent my days locked in, glued to the colors bursting across my screen, slaving away at the same daily rituals.

I had to eat at specific times.

Very specific meals.

In a very specific way.

Or else the dread of my impending doom would drown me.

Would I really spend my entire short-lived spectacle of a life working until my neurons all turned to sludge?

Until my thoughts melted and leaked down onto the freshly mopped floor of some run-of-the-mill desk job?

I imagined myself falling out of the paper-thin windows of our shoebox apartment.

My head cracking open like a hot pocket left in the microwave, blood and sauce spilling onto the pavement.

One of the cleaning robots would scrape my brain matter off the street.

Like scraping eggs off a pan.

I imagined tripping while getting off an auto-bus, leg getting caught in the smart drainage systems on a street, and falling headfirst into a smart trash bin that glitches, misreads me as trash, and closes its lid, crushing my neck and head with enough force to decapitate me.

Death became a constant fixture in my life.

A flicker of movement slips into the corner of my vision.

I turn—

but it’s gone.

My eyes send signals to my brain.But my brain misreads them. Like a machine misreads corrupted data.

The drag on my limbs becomes stronger.

My knees lock up.

System failure.
The snow rushes up to greet me.
